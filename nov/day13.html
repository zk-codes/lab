The Ubiquitous Bar

The navigation bar is everywhere. It sits at the top of nearly every website, a horizontal strip of links promising access to other places. We've come to expect it, rely on it, barely notice it.

But what if it weren't there?

Other Ways to Navigate

Before the nav bar became standard, websites experimented with many wayfinding patterns. Some are worth revisiting:

Contextual links embedded in prose. Breadcrumbs showing your location. Section markers indicating structure. Jump links within the page. The table of contents.

Each method tells you not just where you can go, but where you are. The nav bar floats above the content; these alternatives integrate with it.

Context Over Control

This page uses section markers (the labels above each section), inline waypoints, and return links. Together, they create orientation without a persistent header.

The constraint forces us to ask: what does navigation really need to accomplish? If it's about knowing where you are and where you can go, perhaps the content itself can provide that.

A Different Web

Without the nav bar, pages feel more like documents and less like applications. The scroll becomes the journey. The text guides rather than the chrome.

Perhaps not every page needs this approach. But the constraint reminds us that the nav bar is a choice, not an inevitability.
