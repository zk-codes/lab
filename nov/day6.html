Default State

what your browser decided

You are reading this in your browser's default font. Probably Times New Roman if you're on Windows. Perhaps a system serif on Mac or Linux. I don't know. I've given up control.

No font-family declarations exist on this page. No imported web fonts. No font-size adjustments. The browser's defaults reign supreme.

This text appears exactly as your browser intends. Its size, its shape, its weight - all determined by decades-old defaults.

We spend so much time choosing fonts. Pairing serifs with sans-serifs. Adjusting kerning and tracking. But the browser already made choices for us. Reasonable choices. Tested choices.

The default is not nothing. The default is a decision made by someone else, long ago, that we have collectively agreed to forget.

Before web fonts, this was the web. Every site in Times New Roman, or Arial, or Georgia if the designer was feeling adventurous. And yet the words were read. The ideas transmitted. The internet worked.

The constraint reveals our dependency on typographic control. We believe we need it. But do we? The content still communicates. The hierarchy still exists through headings and emphasis.

This is what undesigned looks like. Or rather, what default-designed looks like. The browser is a designer too. Today, I'm letting it do its job.
